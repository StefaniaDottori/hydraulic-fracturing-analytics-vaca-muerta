# Finding Geological context & data Cost of not knowing
01
Fracturability ≠ Producibility Orgánico vs La Cocina
The Orgánico member has a brittleness index lower than Loma Amarilla, yet delivers the highest initial production — 907 bopd avg IP — because its organic richness (TOC 6.5%) and matrix porosity (ϕ 11.5%) drive fluid storage and connectivity. La Cocina, despite being easier to fracture in isolation, is a ductile, organic-lean formation deposited in an anoxic environment: it breaks, but it doesn't flow. This analysis decouples the mechanical completion target from the actual production target, showing they are not the same zone.
If you don't know this: completion engineers land laterals and concentrate stages in the most brittle intervals — often Loma Amarilla or La Cocina — chasing easy fractures. You get SRV without storage. Wells are completed in the wrong bench, capital is allocated to low-productivity zones, and the portfolio underperforms against type curves with no clear engineering explanation.
02
PDAT fracture half-length is the best predictor of IP r² > 0.70
Cross-correlating PDAT diagnostic half-length against month-1 production across 431 frac stages reveals a statistically significant positive relationship (r² > 0.70). Half-length is the best available field-measurable proxy for Stimulated Reservoir Volume (SRV). A longer propped half-length means more reservoir contacted, more fluid pathways, higher early production. The relationship holds consistently across formations, making it a reliable pre-frac design lever: optimize for half-length, optimize for IP.
If you don't know this: frac design is optimized for net pressure response or treating pressure targets rather than SRV. Teams pump more fluid and proppant without understanding which geometric parameter actually drives production. PDAT data exists in every well file but sits unanalyzed — a data asset generating zero value.
03
La Cocina has a 9% screen-out rate highest across all formations
La Cocina was deposited in an anoxic, deep-water environment with high clay content and low quartz cementation — a depositional context that produces a ductile, plastic rock. Under hydraulic load, fractures don't propagate cleanly: they tortue, branch, and close early, restricting slurry flow and leading to premature screen-out. At 9% screen-out rate across 398 analyzed stages, this formation requires a fundamentally different fluid system (lower viscosity, smaller mesh proppant) and a more conservative rate schedule than the cross-formation default design.
If you don't know this: you apply the same frac recipe across all benches. In La Cocina, this means repeated screen-outs, coiled tubing interventions to clean out bridges, lost stages, and NPT that compounds across a multi-well pad. Each aborted stage is a direct cost. More critically, it becomes a safety risk: wellbore pressure buildup from screen-out events is a leading cause of equipment failures and surface incidents in operations.
04
La Cocina declines 2× faster than Orgánico Di 12%/mo vs 6%/mo
Fitting Arps hyperbolic decline curves (b = 1.2) to 24-month production data by formation shows a stark divergence: Orgánico maintains a decline rate of Di = 6%/month, while La Cocina loses production at Di = 12%/month. By month 12, a La Cocina well produces roughly half what an equivalent Orgánico well would. This is consistent with La Cocina's lower matrix porosity and faster pressure depletion in a ductile, low-storage system. The implication extends beyond production forecasting: it changes the EUR, the economic breakeven, and the optimal restimulation timing for each bench independently.
If you don't know this: reserves are booked using a single basin-wide decline curve. La Cocina wells are systematically over-booked early in field life, with write-downs required as production data matures. Investors and partners receive misleading EUR estimates. Acquisition pricing based on blended type curves overpays for La Cocina-heavy assets — a material financial risk at portfolio scale.

Fracture geometry — models vs. field reality theoretical assumptions vs. what the data actually shows
"Fracture geometry models are hypotheses. Production data is the only real validation."
Classic fracture models — PKN, KGD, radial — are analytical solutions built on idealized assumptions: homogeneous rock, contained fracture height, symmetric propagation, a single planar fracture. In the field, none of these hold. The rock has laminations, natural fractures, lithological variations every few meters, and stress contrasts that shift with depth. What we can actually measure after a treatment is always indirect — and that gap between model and reality is where the risk lives.
# Finding Model applied & assumptions What the model can't see What we used instead
01
Fracturability ≠ Producibility PKN / brittleness index
PKN model assumes a single vertical fracture with contained height propagating in a homogeneous, isotropic rock. Brittleness Index (BI) is used as the primary completion quality proxy — higher BI = better frac candidate. The model predicts longer half-lengths in brittle rock and designs stage placement accordingly.
PKN cannot account for organic content, matrix porosity, or fluid storage capacity. A high-BI formation that breaks cleanly still needs somewhere to store and flow hydrocarbons. In Vaca Muerta, Orgánico has lower BI than Loma Amarilla but higher TOC and porosity — the model ranks it as a worse frac target when it is actually the best producer.
Production data Month-1 IP by formation — the only post-treatment proof that the right zone was targeted.
Geological props TOC, porosity, and permeability per bench correlated against IP to rank true completion quality.
02
PDAT half-length predicts IP model vs. diagnostic
PKN / numerical simulators compute a theoretical fracture half-length from fluid volume, viscosity, and rock mechanical properties. This modeled half-length is what engineers use to claim SRV. It is never directly observed — it is a simulation output with significant uncertainty bounds depending on input quality.
The model assumes perfect proppant transport, no near-wellbore tortuosity, and no fracture complexity. In reality, proppant settles, fractures branch, and part of the designed half-length is never propped. The gap between modeled and PDAT-measured half-length is the single largest source of SRV overestimation in unconventional completions — and it directly explains IP variance between wells with similar designs.
PDAT diagnostic Post-frac pressure transient analysis — gives measured half-length and conductivity, not simulated.
Statistical correlation r² > 0.70 between PDAT half-length and IP across 431 stages — empirical, field-validated.
03
La Cocina: 9% screen-out rate KGD / height containment failure
KGD model assumes a fracture with limited height extension and uniform pressure distribution along its length — appropriate for short, contained fractures in competent rock. For La Cocina, designs based on KGD assume the fracture stays within the target interval, allowing the full slurry volume to be placed before reaching screenout conditions.
La Cocina's ductile, high-clay mineralogy means fractures do not propagate cleanly. Instead of a contained planar fracture, the treatment creates complex, branching geometry with high near-wellbore tortuosity. Pressure builds faster than the model predicts, proppant bridges before the design volume is placed, and the stage screens out. KGD has no mechanism to model this — it cannot simulate ductile fracture complexity or tortuosity-driven pressure escalation.
Screen-out rate 9% observed across 398 La Cocina stages — empirical evidence that model-based designs systematically fail in this formation.
PDAT half-length Shortest measured half-length (163 m avg) confirms fracture complexity and limited propagation despite treatment volume.
04
La Cocina declines 2× faster radial model / EUR overestimation
Radial / pseudo-radial flow models underpin standard Arps decline curve fitting when formation-specific parameters are unavailable. A single basin-wide b-exponent and Di are applied uniformly, assuming that all formations drain similarly once fractured. This is the default approach when no formation-differentiated analysis exists.
La Cocina's ductile matrix has fundamentally different pressure depletion mechanics than Orgánico. A radial model applied uniformly across both formations cannot capture the 2× difference in decline rate. Microseismic data, where available, would show fracture complexity in La Cocina — but even without it, the production signature is unambiguous: the formation depletes faster, and no theoretical model predicted it. Only production data revealed it.
Arps hyperbolic fit Formation-differentiated decline modeling: Di 12%/mo (La Cocina) vs 6%/mo (Orgánico) — b = 1.2, 24-month dataset.
Production data The only real post-treatment validation available — no microseismic, no tracers, no imaging. Data speaks.

02
PDAT half-length predicts IP underutilized diagnostic
Without half-length as a design target, pumping schedules are not optimized for SRV. Engineers either over-pump (excess fluid, pressure buildup risk) or under-pump (insufficient fracture propagation, low IP). Both outcomes generate unnecessary field events.
Over-pumping scenarios: unplanned pressure exceedances trigger emergency rate reductions — standby hours while the crew stabilizes wellbore pressure and evaluates surface equipment integrity. PDAT data already exists in every well file but requires analyst time to interpret.
HHP (hydraulic horsepower) Excess pump rate requires additional HHP on location — idle pump trucks on standby generate day-rate cost.
Friction reducers Higher-than-needed slurry rates require increased FR concentration to control treating pressure.
03
La Cocina: 9% screen-out rate highest operational risk
A screen-out event in La Cocina means the proppant slurry bridges in the near-wellbore or fracture throat before the designed volume is placed. The stage is aborted. The wellbore must be cleaned out before the next stage can begin — adding unplanned hours to the pad schedule.
Each screen-out event generates: coiled tubing (CT) mobilization for bridge cleanout (4–8 hrs typical NPT), pump shutdown and wellbore pressure bleed-off time, crew standby while CT unit rigged in, and potential re-perforation if the interval is damaged. At 9% rate across a 15-stage lateral, expect 1–2 CT interventions per well.
Extra pump unit Standby high-pressure pump required on location for pressure control during screen-out event management.
Friction reducers Increased FR dosage needed to reduce tortuosity and near-wellbore friction — unplanned chemical volume.
Coiled tubing unit CT mobilization per screen-out event — day-rate plus crew standby hours.
Surface iron Pressure spike risk during screen-out requires check of all surface iron integrity — potential inspection downtime.
04
La Cocina declines 2× faster reserves & restimulation timing
Faster decline means the economic limit is reached sooner than planned. Restimulation or infill drilling decisions must be made earlier. If operations teams are working from a single basin type curve, La Cocina wells trigger unplanned interventions — mobilizing equipment that wasn't scheduled in the annual operations plan.
Unplanned restimulation jobs carry full standby overhead: pump fleet, blender, data acquisition unit, and wireline crew must be re-mobilized outside the scheduled pad campaign. Off-cycle mobilization is consistently more expensive than planned-sequence operations. Standby hours accumulate during logistics coordination for an unscheduled intervention.
